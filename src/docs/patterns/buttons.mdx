# Variations

The appearance of a button depends on available space, the attention required from the user, and what action it triggers.

Brightlayer UI supports five different button styles.

## Floating Action Buttons (FABs)

Floating action buttons, commonly known as “FAB”, are occasionally used on mobile apps. They perform the primary or most common action on a screen. FABs are usually pinned to the bottom right corner of the screen so that users have constant access to these buttons no matter how far they have scrolled down the screen.

<ImageGrid
    images={[FABMobile1, FABMobile2]}

        `A FAB in a user list for adding new users. When a FAB icon is a "universal icon" and its meaning can be clearly identified by the user, the button label may be omitted.`,
    ]}
/>

Be careful when using FABs, as they can be visually disconnected from the user's workflow.

<ImageGrid
    images={[FABDesktop, FABEmptyState]}

    ]}
/>

## Colors

By default, buttons should use the primary theme color. Consider coloring buttons with destructive actions in red to alert the user.

<ImageGrid images={[RedButton]} captionsUnderImages={[`DO: Use red colors in buttons for destructive actions.`]} />

## Shapes

The default shape of a Brightlayer UI button is a rectangle with rounded corners of radius 4dp. Alternatively, consumer-facing applications may also consider using round / pill-shaped buttons consistently throughout the entire product.

You should place buttons in the order you want them to be read. Most languages are read from left to right and from top to bottom.

## Button Labels

Buttons are meant to be actions, and thus their labels should be verbs.

<ImageGrid
    images={[LabelLogin, LabelLogIn]}
    captionsUnderImages={[`DONT: “Login” is a noun, not a verb.`, `DO: Use the verb form “Log In” instead.`]}
/>

How you choose between Title Case and Sentence case depends on [your app's formality and your user's cultural background](https://uxdesign.cc/title-case-vs-sentence-case-in-ux-writing-212087192261). You should ensure that your selection stays consistent throughout the entire application.

# Responsive

There are some special considerations when designing for smaller-sized screens. For example, when buttons or [steppers](/patterns/steppers) are pinned to the bottom of the screen, the phone’s keyboard, when invoked, should push them up.

<ImageGrid images={[ResponsiveFooterPushedUp]} captionsUnderImages={[`Mobile phone’s keyboard pushes buttons up.`]} />

Buttons should stay within a device's safe area.

<ImageGrid
    images={[ResponsiveBottomSlider1, ResponsiveBottomSlider2]}
    captionsUnderImages={[
        `DONT: Do not place buttons behind or too close to iPhone’s bottom slider.`,
        `DO: Place buttons above iPhone’s bottom slider.`,
    ]}
/>

Mobile phones have limited horizontal screen space. If your app supports multiple languages, the length of button labels might be wider than the primary language you are designing for. As a result, you should always consider using stacked buttons on mobile.

<ImageGrid
    images={[ResponsiveStackedMobile, ResponsiveStackedDesktop]}
    captionsUnderImages={[
        `Use stacked buttons on mobile apps.`,
        `Use side-by-side buttons on desktop or tablet apps, if there's enough horizontal space.`,
    ]}
/>

HMIs (Human-Machine Interfaces) are tablets commonly mounted on machines, workstations, and walls. Users often find it hard to aim at buttons as precisely as with other screens, and to worsen the situation, they may be wearing gloves while interacting with the screen. As a result, buttons on HMIs are usually larger.

<ImageGrid
    images={[ResponsiveHMI]}
    regularWidth
    caption={`Buttons on HMIs (#1) are physically larger than buttons on mobile phones (#2).`}
/>
